Early Extubation Resulting in Shorter Time to Post-Op Feeding


Exploratory Data Analysis

We will begin the exploratory analysis by reviewing the summary statistics on the df_final dataset (n = 322) from the Pediatric Heart Network in order to identify if early extubation has any association with shorter time to the first postoperative oral feeding, and whether this differed based on the diagnosis group, tetralogy of fallout or coarctation of the aorta.

Based on the final dataset, the average age of the individual undergoing surgery was 115.14 days, or 0.32 years. The data was relatively clean and the final dataset resulted in no missingness. The summary statistics show that the average number of days to first postoperative oral feeding is 1.90 days, with a standard deviation of 4.44 days. The percent of surgical procedures that had a diagnosis of tetralogy of fallout is 62.82% and coarctation of the aorta is 37.18%. The percent of surgical procedures that experienced early extubation (extubation within 6 hours of surgery) is 53.79% and those that did not experience early extubation is 46.21%.

Conlusion


Based on the model results, we can suggest that early extubation is associated with shorter time to postoperative oral feeding while holding for diagnosis type. Additionally, the diagnosis group also display statistical significance based on reducing the time to the first postoperative oral feed. This conclusion was drawn from the p-value for both outcomes falling below the threshold of significance (< 0.05) along with a 95% confidence interval not including 1 in either outcome.

Based on the risk ratio, we identified that individuals undergoing early extubation had 0.31 times the rate of incidence for reducing time to postoperative oral feeding and individuals within the tetralogy of fallout diagnosis group had 0.72 times the rate of incidence for reducing time to postoperative oral feeding.
